Range MSO LLC — The Holding Entity

Range MSO LLC is a Management Services Organization — a structure specifically designed for healthcare. The MSO owns everything except the medical license itself: the software, the equipment, the brand, the clinical protocols, and the management contracts with physician-owned practices.

This structure matters because it separates business value from clinical liability. If a malpractice claim is filed, it hits the medical PC — not the MSO that holds the assets. More importantly for investors, MSOs trade at 8–12x EBITDA multiples in M&A, compared to 1–2x for standalone medical practices. The reason: an MSO's revenue is contractual (via management services agreements), its assets are transferable, and it can scale across locations without requiring each buyer to hold a medical license.

Investor takeaway: Your equity is in the entity that holds all scalable, transferable value — not in a physician-dependent practice.

Range System — Proprietary Software

Range System is a custom-built clinic management platform developed in-house. It handles patient intake, protocol management, lab result ingestion, service logging, consent workflows, and the patient portal. Unlike off-the-shelf EMR software, it's purpose-built for regenerative medicine clinics and fully owned by the MSO.

The software creates a SaaS licensing opportunity: each new clinic location (whether owned or under management agreement) pays a software license fee on top of the standard management services fee. This dual-revenue layer — management fee plus software fee — increases per-location economics and makes each new location more valuable to the MSO.

Investor takeaway: Proprietary software owned at the MSO level means recurring license revenue per location, increasing margins as the network grows.

Range Recovery Method — Clinical Intellectual Property

The Range Recovery Method is a proprietary set of clinical protocols covering hormone replacement, peptide therapy, weight management, IV therapy, and regenerative treatments. These protocols were developed within the Range Medical system and represent the clinic's differentiated clinical approach.

Critically, this IP is vested in the MSO, not in any individual physician or member. This means the clinical methodology transfers cleanly in an acquisition — a buyer gets the protocols, the training framework, and the clinical playbook as part of the MSO purchase. There is no key-person risk on the IP itself.

Investor takeaway: Clinical IP vested in the MSO means it travels with the entity in a sale — no renegotiation, no key-person dependency.

Equipment Portfolio — MSO-Level Ownership

All major clinical equipment — hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBOT) chambers, red light therapy devices, DEXA body composition scanners, and IV therapy stations — is owned directly by the MSO and leased to the medical PCs under the management services agreement.

This structure serves two purposes. First, it insulates equipment assets from clinical liability — a malpractice claim against the PC cannot reach the MSO's equipment. Second, it consolidates all capital assets on one balance sheet, making the MSO's asset position cleaner for valuation and easier to finance against.

Investor takeaway: Equipment owned at the MSO level is protected from clinical liability and consolidated for cleaner valuation.

Management Services Agreement (MSA)

The MSA is the contractual backbone of the MSO model. Each physician-owned medical PC enters into a long-term management services agreement with Range MSO LLC. Under this agreement, the MSO provides all non-clinical services — software, equipment, billing, marketing, compliance infrastructure, staffing, and operational management — in exchange for a management fee (typically a percentage of collected revenue).

Fees flow up from the PC to the MSO. Services flow down from the MSO to the PC. This creates a clean, contractual revenue stream for the MSO that is predictable, recurring, and not dependent on any single physician's continued participation.

Investor takeaway: The MSA creates recurring contractual revenue for the MSO — the entity you own equity in — from every active clinic location.

Newport Beach Medical PC

The Newport Beach location is the operating flagship of the Range Medical network. It is structured as a physician-owned professional corporation (PC), with Dr. Damien Burgess as the physician of record. California's corporate practice of medicine doctrine requires that medical practices be owned by licensed physicians — the PC satisfies this requirement.

The PC handles all clinical operations: patient care, prescribing, medical decision-making, and regulatory compliance. All non-clinical functions — software, equipment, marketing, billing, HR, operations — are provided by Range MSO LLC under the management services agreement. The MSO charges a management fee for these services, which creates the MSO's revenue stream.

Investor takeaway: The PC is the clinical shell. The MSO captures the business economics through the management fee structure.

How Decisions Work at Range MSO

The governance framework is designed to balance two things investors care about: speed (day-to-day decisions happen fast, without committee bottleneck) and protection (major decisions that affect equity value require broad consent). Every decision the MSO faces enters this flow.

Investor takeaway: This isn't governance theater — it's a practical system that keeps the business moving while protecting your position on decisions that matter.

What Counts as a "Major Decision"

The operating agreement specifically defines five categories of major decisions: (1) sale or disposition of substantial MSO assets, (2) admitting new members or issuing additional equity, (3) incurring debt exceeding $100,000, (4) voluntary dissolution of the MSO, and (5) amending the operating agreement itself.

These categories are chosen because they're the decisions that directly affect your ownership stake, your dilution exposure, the MSO's debt profile, and its continued existence. Everything else is an operational decision handled through the fast-path system below.

Investor takeaway: The "major decision" threshold is specifically drawn around the actions that could dilute you, leverage the entity beyond comfort, or change the deal you agreed to.

Fast Path — Single Domain Authority

When a decision falls clearly within one Co-Managing Member's domain, that person has full authority to act without seeking approval from the other managing member or from the broader membership. This is the "fast path" — the default for the vast majority of day-to-day decisions.

Examples: Chris approves a new software feature, hires an operations manager, or negotiates a marketing contract. Damien approves a new clinical protocol, credentials a nurse practitioner, or selects a medical supply vendor. Neither needs to wait for the other.

Investor takeaway: The business runs without bottleneck on routine decisions. Speed is an asset, not a risk, when domains are clearly defined.

Joint Approval — Cross-Domain Decisions

When a decision spans both domains — for example, purchasing new clinical equipment (Chris's procurement authority) that changes a clinical protocol (Damien's medical authority) — both Co-Managing Members must agree. This prevents one side from unilaterally making decisions that materially affect the other's domain.

If the two Co-Managing Members reach a deadlock on a cross-domain operational decision, it escalates to a member vote decided by simple majority of ownership units. This ensures no operational decision can be permanently blocked by disagreement between two people.

Investor takeaway: Cross-domain decisions require partnership, but deadlock has a clear resolution path — no decision stays stuck.

For any major decision, both Co-Managing Members — Chris and Damien — must provide written approval. Verbal agreements are not sufficient. This requirement creates a clear paper trail and ensures both operational leaders have consciously evaluated and agreed to the action before it proceeds.

70% Supermajority Member Vote

After both Co-Managing Members approve in writing, the decision must also pass a vote of members holding at least 70% of total membership units. With the current cap table (three 30% holders and two 5% holders), this means at least two of the three 30% holders must vote in favor, plus at least one 5% holder if needed to clear 70%.

The 70% threshold is deliberately set above a simple majority to prevent any two members from pushing through major decisions without broader consensus — but below unanimity to prevent a single minority holder from blocking legitimate action.

Investor takeaway: The 70% threshold means your equity can't be diluted, the entity can't take on major debt, and the agreement can't be changed without supermajority consent that includes people beyond just the managing members.

Deadlock Resolution — Major Decisions

If a major decision cannot clear both gates (managing member written approval + 70% member vote), the operating agreement provides a structured escalation path. First, the parties enter non-binding mediation in Orange County, California. Mediation is designed to resolve disputes without litigation, preserving business relationships and avoiding the cost and distraction of court.

If mediation fails to resolve the deadlock, the matter proceeds to judicial resolution in Orange County state or federal court. Specifying the jurisdiction in advance prevents forum-shopping and ensures any dispute is resolved in the MSO's home jurisdiction.

Investor takeaway: There is no scenario where a major decision is permanently stuck. The path from deadlock to resolution is defined, jurisdictionally specific, and designed to protect all members equally.

Transfer Restrictions

No membership interest may be transferred without joint approval of both Co-Managing Members plus a 70% supermajority member vote. Before any approved transfer, existing members have a 30-day right of first refusal to purchase the interest on the same terms offered by the outside buyer.

This prevents unwanted third parties from entering the cap table and gives existing members the first opportunity to consolidate ownership.

Buy-Sell on Exit

Upon a triggering event (death, disability, voluntary withdrawal, or involuntary removal), the MSO has a 90-day buyout option to purchase the departing member's interest at fair market value determined by an independent certified appraiser (CBV or ABV credentialed).

Payment terms: 24-month structured payout at the Wall Street Journal prime rate. This protects the MSO from a sudden cash drain while ensuring the departing member receives fair value on a defined timeline.

Independent appraisal eliminates negotiation — the price is what a credentialed third party says it's worth, not what either side argues for.
