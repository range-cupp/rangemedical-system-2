The Six Your PCP Probably Skipped

1. ApoB

ApoB measures the actual number of atherogenic particles in your blood—the ones that drive plaque. It is a better predictor of cardiovascular events than LDL cholesterol, full stop. The National Lipid Association said so officially in 2024. Your doctor is running LDL because that's what's in the standard panel. He's not running ApoB unless you ask.

Standard: 55–140 mg/dL · Optimal (primary prevention): <90 mg/dL · Higher-risk: <70 mg/dL · Aggressive: <60 mg/dL

2. Lipoprotein(a)—Lp(a)

Lp(a) is a genetically determined particle that drives cardiovascular and aortic valve disease independent of everything else you do. It is roughly 70–90% genetic, meaning you can be a marathoner who eats clean and still have a 3x cardiovascular risk because of one number you've never had measured. The American Heart Association told doctors in 2022 to start checking it. Most still don't.

Risk threshold: ≥50 mg/dL (or ≥125 nmol/L) per ACC/AHA. You measure it once in your life. You never have to measure it again.

3. Total and Free Testosterone (with SHBG)

The standard testosterone test is total T. The lab reports "normal" as anything above 264 ng/dL based on a 2018 Endocrine Society guideline harmonized to healthy young men. Two problems: first, "healthy young men" is not "you at 48," and second, total T doesn't tell you what's actually bioavailable. Free testosterone—calculated from total T and SHBG—does. Most PCPs don't run SHBG. Some don't even run free T.

Standard total T: 264–916 ng/dL · Where guys feel good: Total T 700–1000+ with free T in the upper third of range.

4. Fasting Insulin (and HOMA-IR)

A1c and fasting glucose tell you whether you're diabetic right now. Fasting insulin tells you what your pancreas has been doing for the last decade to keep you out of diabetes. HOMA-IR is the calculated index that combines them. Insulin resistance shows up on this number 10 to 20 years before A1c moves.

Standard: ~2.5–13 μIU/mL · Optimal: <7, ideally <5 · HOMA-IR: <2.0 no IR · 2.0–2.9 early IR · ≥3.0 significant IR

5. hs-CRP

High-sensitivity C-reactive protein is a marker of systemic inflammation. The JUPITER trial in 2008 established it as an independent predictor of cardiovascular events even when LDL was normal. The AHA uses three buckets: low, average, and high risk.

Buckets: <1.0 mg/L low risk · 1.0–3.0 average · >3.0 high · Two readings two weeks apart tells you about your baseline.

6. Estradiol (yes, estradiol)

Estrogen isn't a "women's hormone." Men make it from testosterone via aromatase, and it does real work—bone density, libido, cognition. Too low is as bad as too high. Most men on TRT find this out the hard way. Most PCPs don't measure it.

Optimal for men: ~20–40 pg/mL (sensitive assay). If you're considering testosterone optimization, this is the number that determines whether you'll feel great or feel like you have PMS.

4

The Advanced Six—For Optimizing, Not Just Surviving

The first six tell you whether something is wrong. These six tell you whether you're optimizing or coasting.

Free T3 and Free T4

Most PCPs check TSH and call it a thyroid panel. TSH is the brain's signal to the thyroid—not the thyroid's output. You can have normal TSH and a sluggish thyroid that's tanking your energy, mood, and metabolism. Free T3 and free T4 measure the actual hormones doing the work.

Optimal Free T3: upper third of range, typically 3.5–4.2 pg/mL.

DHEA-S

The mother hormone for testosterone and estrogen. Drops 80% between age 25 and 75. Most PCPs never look at it.

Optimal: Top quartile for age, typically 300–500 μg/dL for men over 40.

Cortisol (AM)

If you're waking at 3am, it's probably this. Morning cortisol tells you whether your HPA axis is functioning. Chronically elevated or chronically suppressed both kill recovery, sleep, and body composition.

Optimal AM: 10–18 μg/dL drawn before 9am.

Vitamin D (25-OH)

Boring marker, real impact. Most men in Newport Beach are deficient anyway—sounds insane in a sunny climate, but it's the truth. Drives bone, mood, immune function, testosterone production.

Optimal: 50–80 ng/mL. Lab "normal" of 30+ is the floor, not the goal.

Ferritin

Iron storage. Too low and you're exhausted. Too high (which is more common in men) and you may have hemochromatosis driving cardiovascular and liver risk.

Optimal: 50–150 ng/mL. Anything over 300 in a man warrants a workup.

Homocysteine

Marker of methylation status. Elevated homocysteine independently predicts cardiovascular events and cognitive decline. Driven largely by B-vitamin status—fixable with the right protocol.

Optimal: <8 μmol/L.

What Nobody Tells You About Reading Your Own Labs

This is the page everyone wants to skip. Don't.

"Normal" is not "optimal," and the gap is where the decline lives.

Lab reference ranges are built from population averages. The population is fat, tired, hormonally suppressed, and inflamed. If you anchor yourself to "normal," you're benchmarking against the people who feel like you do. The whole point of advanced bloodwork is to see how far you are from optimal—not how close you are to disease.

Self-medicating off Reddit doses is how guys end up with hematocrit at 56% and a stroke risk they didn't know they had.

You've Googled the protocols. You've seen the threads. You know guys who buy testosterone from a peptide site and inject themselves on a schedule a guy with no medical license recommended. Some of them are fine. Some of them have hematocrit so elevated their blood is sludge. Some have estradiol high enough to grow breast tissue. Some have suppressed their own production so completely they can't reverse it. The labs in this guide are what prevent all three of those.

Lp(a) is genetic. If yours is high, your kids' may be too.

This is the one nobody talks about. If you find out at 48 that your Lp(a) is 80 mg/dL and you have a 3x cardiovascular risk you've been carrying your entire life, your kids likely inherited the same gene. Measure them in their 20s. Catching it early changes the trajectory.

"I feel fine" is not a substitute for bloodwork.

The Newport Beach Guy is famous for this. He feels fine—until the cardiologist says he had a silent MI two years ago and didn't notice. Or the urologist tells him his testosterone has been in the gutter for a decade and that's why his marriage has been weird. The first symptom of most decline is the absence of symptoms. By the time something hurts, you've been losing ground for years.

"Why Isn't My Doctor Running These?"

Fair question. The short answer is: insurance, time, and inertia. The longer answer is more useful.

Insurance

Most of these markers—ApoB, Lp(a), fasting insulin, free testosterone, SHBG, hs-CRP—are not covered by standard insurance unless you already have a diagnosis that justifies them. Which means your doctor can order them, but you might get a $400 bill in the mail. Most PCPs default to what insurance covers, which is the basic panel from 1985.

Time

Your PCP has 12 minutes with you. He's catching disease, not optimizing your physiology. He's not trained to read free T3 in the upper-third-of-range as "this is why you feel slow." He's trained to read it as "not hypothyroid, all good."

Inertia

Medical guidelines update slowly. ApoB has been a better marker than LDL for 30+ years. The 2024 NLA consensus made it official. Your PCP's panel hasn't caught up. It probably won't for another five years.

None of this is your doctor's fault. It's a structural problem. He's working inside a system designed for sick-care, not optimization. If you want optimization, you have to go outside that system. That's it.
